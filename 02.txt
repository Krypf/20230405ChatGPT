A.

David Hume was an 18th-century Scottish philosopher who made significant contributions to our understanding of causality. Hume's theory of causality is based on his skepticism towards the idea that we can know the necessary connection between cause and effect. He argued that our belief in causality is based on habit and custom, rather than on rational knowledge or observation. Here are some of the key steps involved in understanding Hume's theory of causality:

Impressions and ideas: According to Hume, all of our knowledge comes from experience. He distinguished between two types of experience: impressions and ideas. Impressions are the immediate, vivid sensations we receive from the world around us, while ideas are the copies of these impressions that we retain in our minds.

Constant conjunction: Hume observed that we often see one event (the cause) followed by another event (the effect). He called this "constant conjunction." For example, we might observe that striking a match causes it to light. We come to expect that the same effect will occur every time we strike a match.

Association: Hume argued that our belief in causality is based on the association we make between the cause and the effect. We come to expect the effect to follow the cause, based on our past experience of constant conjunction. This association is a product of our imagination, rather than of any rational knowledge or observation.

Necessary connection: Hume denied that there is any necessary connection between cause and effect. He argued that we cannot observe or infer any such connection. Instead, we simply expect the effect to follow the cause, based on our habit and custom of seeing constant conjunction.

Problem of induction: Hume's theory of causality raises a problem for induction, which is the process of generalizing from particular observations to general principles. Hume argued that induction is not rationally justified, because there is no logical connection between the observed events and the inferred principles. Therefore, he concluded that induction is based on habit and custom, rather than on rational knowledge.

Hume's theory of causality has been widely discussed and debated in philosophy and science. Some scholars have criticized his skepticism towards causality, while others have defended it as a useful corrective to dogmatic beliefs.
