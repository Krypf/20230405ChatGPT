Q.
Showing references, explain step by step the definition of causality.

A.
The concept of causality is fundamental in science and philosophy, and has been the subject of much debate throughout history. There are different approaches to defining causality, but one commonly accepted definition is the following:

"Causality is the relationship between an event (the cause) and a second event (the effect), where the second event is a consequence of the first."

This definition suggests that causality involves a temporal ordering of events, where the cause precedes the effect, and a necessary connection between them, where the effect would not have occurred without the cause.

Here are some of the key steps involved in understanding and defining causality:

Temporal precedence: To establish causality, it is necessary to show that the cause precedes the effect in time. This means that the cause must come before the effect, so that the effect is not mistaken for the cause. For example, if a researcher wants to establish whether a particular drug causes a particular side effect, they need to show that the drug was administered before the side effect occurred.

Association: Once temporal precedence is established, the next step is to show that the cause and effect are associated with each other. This means that there is a relationship between the cause and the effect, and that the effect is more likely to occur when the cause is present. For example, if a researcher wants to establish whether smoking causes lung cancer, they need to show that smokers are more likely to develop lung cancer than non-smokers.

Plausibility: The third step involves showing that the relationship between the cause and effect is plausible. This means that there is a biological, physical, or theoretical mechanism that could explain how the cause leads to the effect. For example, if a researcher wants to establish whether a particular gene causes a particular disease, they need to show that the gene is involved in a biological process that could lead to the disease.

Coherence: The fourth step involves showing that the relationship between the cause and effect is coherent with other established knowledge. This means that the relationship is consistent with other known facts and theories. For example, if a researcher wants to establish whether a particular environmental toxin causes a particular birth defect, they need to show that the relationship is consistent with what is known about the effects of toxins on development.

Experimentation: Finally, the fifth step involves conducting experiments to test the hypothesis that the cause leads to the effect. This means that the researcher manipulates the cause and observes the effect, while controlling for other factors that might influence the outcome. For example, if a researcher wants to establish whether a particular educational intervention causes improved academic performance, they might randomly assign some students to receive the intervention and others to receive no intervention, and then compare their academic performance.

In summary, the definition of causality involves a temporal ordering of events, an association between the cause and effect, a plausible mechanism, coherence with other knowledge, and experimentation to test the hypothesis.
